Section 01
Empathy Maps
Understanding the lived experience of our primary users — what they say, think, do, and feel when facing cognitive challenges.
◈
Person with ADHD
Executive function, focus, task initiation
Says
"I'll do it later"
"I forgot again"
"I just can't start"
Thinks
"Why can't I just DO the thing?"
"Everyone else manages fine"
"Maybe if I reorganize my list again..."
Does
Opens 15 tabs simultaneously
Rewrites to-do lists repeatedly
Starts tasks then abandons them
Hyperfocuses on wrong things
Avoids "boring" tasks entirely
Feels
Frustrated by gap between intention and action
Ashamed of unfinished work
Overwhelmed by large undefined tasks
Relieved when someone breaks things down
◆
Person with Autism
Structure, predictability, sensory needs
Says
"What exactly do you mean?"
"Can you be more specific?"
"I need to know the steps"
Thinks
"This is too vague"
"Why did they change the interface?"
"I need to prepare for this"
Does
Creates rigid routines
Avoids ambiguous instructions
Gets stressed by UI changes
Prefers written over verbal
Feels
Anxious when routines break
Safe in structured environments
Overwhelmed by sensory-heavy interfaces
Calm with predictable systems
◇
Person with Dyslexia
Reading processing, text comprehension
Says
"Can you explain that simpler?"
"I read it three times and still don't get it"
"Too many words"
Thinks
"This document is a wall of text"
"I'm not stupid, I just process differently"
"I wish there was an audio version"
Does
Avoids long documents
Uses text-to-speech tools
Re-reads paragraphs multiple times
Prefers bullet points and visuals
Feels
Exhausted by dense text
Embarrassed asking for simpler versions
Relieved when info is clear and short
Confident when they understand quickly
◉
Person with Anxiety
Decision paralysis, avoidance, reassurance
Says
"What if I do it wrong?"
"There's too much to do"
"I don't know where to start"
Thinks
"Everything is urgent"
"If I miss this deadline everything falls apart"
"I need to check one more time"
Does
Avoids starting tasks
Overthinks simple decisions
Seeks constant reassurance
Freezes with long task lists
Feels
Paralyzed by too many options
Guilty about procrastination
Calmed by clear single next steps
Triggered by urgency language and red badges
Section 02
Persona Spectrum
Using Microsoft's Inclusive Design framework to show how designing for permanent needs extends to temporary and situational contexts.
Need Permanent Temporary Situational
Cognitive overload ADHD Concussion, sleep deprivation Finals week, new parent
Structured info need Autism Language learner in new system Reading legal docs for first time
Reading barrier Dyslexia Eye strain / injury Phone in bright sunlight
Decision paralysis Anxiety disorder Burnout, grief Complex project, no clear start
Designing for permanent disabilities extends benefits to 10x more people with temporary and situational needs. Every feature in pebble serves all three columns. Reference: Microsoft Inclusive Design Toolkit — inclusive.microsoft.design

Section 04
Problem Statements
The "How Might We" questions that drive every design decision in pebble.
How might we reduce the cognitive load of starting a task for someone whose brain makes starting the hardest part?
How might we simplify academic documents without making the reader feel talked down to?
How might we provide social study motivation without the sensory overwhelm of video calls?
How might we build an AI assistant that people with anxiety can actually trust — one that never adds pressure?
Section 05
Design Principles
The five commitments that shape every feature, interaction, and word in pebble.
01
⚙
The app adapts to your brain, not the other way around
Every feature respects individual cognitive preferences
02
✓
Small steps count just as much as big ones
Progress is measured in movement, not perfection
03
☼
Calm is a feature, not a limitation
No urgency, no guilt, no comparison, no streaks
04
☁
Show your work, Pebble
Every AI decision is explainable and reversible
05
✺
Solve for one, extend to many
Designing for ADHD helps anyone who's overwhelmed
